How to Stop Foreclosure
in California

If you're searching for how to stop foreclosure — or just trying to understand how much time you have left — you're in the right place. Here's the honest answer, and what you can actually do right now.

California is a non-judicial foreclosure state — meaning foreclosures do not go through the court system. This makes the process significantly faster than many people expect. Understanding the timeline is the first step toward knowing how to stop foreclosure before it's too late.

The good news: there are real ways to stop foreclosure at almost every stage of the process — but the options available to you depend heavily on where you are in the timeline. The earlier you act, the more doors are open.

The California Foreclosure Timeline — Step by Step

  • 1
    Missed Payments (Day 1–90)Your lender will begin contacting you. After 30 days, missed payments may be reported to credit bureaus. You're still in a relatively strong position here — the more options you have.
  • 2
    Notice of Default Filed (Day 90+)After roughly 90 days of missed payments, your lender can record a Notice of Default (NOD) with the county. This is public record. The 90-day reinstatement clock starts here — you can still stop foreclosure by paying everything you owe.
  • 3
    Notice of Trustee Sale (Day 180+)After the 90-day reinstatement period, the lender can record a Notice of Trustee Sale. The auction must be scheduled at least 21 days from this notice.
  • 4
    Foreclosure Auction (Day 111–200+)The home is sold to the highest bidder at a public auction. Once this happens, your options change significantly — though they don't disappear entirely (see surplus funds).
⏰ The Key Number: 111 Days

From the moment a Notice of Default is filed, you may have as little as 111 days before the foreclosure sale. That's not much time — but it's often enough to act, especially in the earlier stages. The sooner you reach out for help, the more doors are open.

What Can I Do at Each Stage?

Stage 1 — Missed Payments (Before NOD)

This is your strongest position. Options include calling your lender's loss mitigation department to ask about forbearance or a repayment plan, applying for a loan modification, or exploring whether selling the property makes sense for your situation.

Stage 2 — After Notice of Default

You still have options, but the clock is running. You can reinstate the loan by paying everything owed plus fees. You can continue negotiating with your lender. You can sell the property — and if there's equity, you walk away with money. You can explore a short sale if you owe more than the home is worth.

Stage 3 — After Notice of Trustee Sale

The window is narrow but not closed. In some cases, lenders will still negotiate. A cash buyer who can close quickly may be able to purchase the property before the auction date. Some homeowners file for bankruptcy at this stage to trigger an automatic stay — this pauses foreclosure temporarily but is a serious step that requires an attorney.

Stage 4 — After the Auction

Once the home is sold, you no longer own it. However, if the auction sale price exceeded what you owed, you may be entitled to the difference — called surplus funds. This is more common in Alameda County than most people realize. Learn more about claiming surplus funds here.

How to Stop Foreclosure in California — Your Real Options

If you're asking how to stop foreclosure, the answer depends on which stage you're in. Here's what actually works at each point:

  • Reinstate the loan — Pay everything past due including fees. This immediately stops foreclosure and restores your loan to good standing. Available up until 5 business days before the auction.
  • Negotiate a loan modification — Ask your lender's loss mitigation department to permanently change your loan terms so payments become manageable going forward.
  • Request forbearance — A temporary pause or reduction in payments. Buys time but doesn't resolve the underlying issue.
  • Sell the property before the auction — If you have equity, selling to a cash buyer who can close quickly is one of the most reliable ways to stop foreclosure and walk away with money in hand. We can often close in time even when the auction date feels very close.
  • Pursue a short sale — If you owe more than the home is worth, your lender may agree to accept less than the full balance. Requires lender approval and takes time.
  • File for bankruptcy — An automatic stay immediately halts foreclosure proceedings. This is a significant legal step that requires an attorney and has long-term consequences — but it can stop the clock while you sort out next steps.
  • Work with a HUD-approved housing counselor — Free service, available at 1-800-569-4287. They can often negotiate directly with lenders on your behalf.
