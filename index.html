Behind on Property Taxes / Tax Lien
"I owe back taxes and I'm afraid I'll lose my home to the county."
In California, if property taxes go unpaid for 5 years, the county can sell the property at a tax defaulted auction. But there are options before it gets there — including selling the home yourself before the auction date and paying off the tax debt from the proceeds. You keep the equity. You stay in control. Know Your Foreclosure Timeline in California
California is a non-judicial foreclosure state, which means it moves faster than most people expect. Notice of Default filed → 90 days → Notice of Trustee Sale posted → 21 days → Foreclosure auction. That's roughly 111 days from start to finish. If you've already received a Notice of Default, your window is narrowing. Call us today — even a 15-minute conversation could change your outcome.
Did You Lose Your Home to Foreclosure?

You May Have Money Waiting for You — and Not Know It.

"Most people walk away from foreclosure thinking they've lost everything. But if your home sold for more than you owed — the excess belongs to you by law."

When a home is foreclosed and sold at a trustee auction, the lender is only entitled to recover what they were owed — the loan balance, fees, and costs. If the property sells for more than that amount, the remaining money is called foreclosure surplus funds — and it legally belongs to the former homeowner.

In California, these funds are held by the county trustee or the court. There is a claims process to get that money back — but it has deadlines, paperwork, and legal steps that most people don't know how to navigate. The money doesn't come to you automatically. You have to claim it.

We can help you find out whether surplus funds exist in your case, understand the claims process, and connect you with the right resources to recover what's yours. This is especially common in Alameda County, where rising property values have meant that many foreclosure auction sales have exceeded the debt owed.

How It Works
1
Your Home Sells at Auction
The trustee sells the property to the highest bidder. If the sale price exceeds your loan balance and fees, a surplus exists.
2
Funds Are Held by the County
The surplus is deposited with the county or court — not automatically returned to you. Most former homeowners never learn it exists.
3
You Have a Window to Claim It
California law gives you a limited time to file a claim. After that window closes, the funds may be forfeited to the state. Acting quickly matters.
